Speaking Up & Interjecting

Claim space in conversations gracefully without being rude. Today you'll learn 5 interjection patterns to enter conversations naturally, build on others' ideas, and contribute strategically.

Interjecting
60 minutes
5 patterns
Meeting Presence
💡 C1 Insight
"B2 speakers either stay silent or apologize for speaking: 'Sorry to interrupt, but...' C1 speakers enter conversations strategically: 'Building on what Sarah just said, I'd add...' — confident, collaborative, and value-adding. The difference isn't vocabulary — it's presence."

Today's Philosophy

From Interruption to Strategic Entry

Most people either stay silent (miss opportunity) or interrupt rudely (damage relationship). C1 communicators understand that interjecting is a skill — not an interruption. They know how to enter conversations gracefully by building on others' ideas, not competing with them. Professional presence isn't about talking the most. It's about contributing strategically.

Why most people struggle to speak up
  • Wait for "perfect" moment that never comes
  • Apologize: "Sorry to interrupt, but..."
  • Interrupt without acknowledging others
  • Dominate without building on ideas
  • Stay silent and resent it later
What C1 communicators do differently
  • Build on others: "Building on what [Name] said..."
  • Request space politely: "Can I jump in here?"
  • Signal intention: "I'd like to add something..."
  • Share brief insights: "One thing I'd add..."
  • Contribute quickly: "Just one quick thought..."

5 Interjection Patterns

5 patterns để enter conversations gracefully. Mỗi pattern có life & business examples + alternatives. Click 🔊 để nghe phát âm.

1
"Building on what [Name] just said..."
/ˈbɪldɪŋ ɒn wɒt [neɪm] dʒʌst sɛd/
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High
Casual → Professional
Acknowledge + add value. Show you're building on their idea, not replacing it.
Why C1?

"Building on" = collaborative framing. Shows you listened and are adding, not competing. Creates sense of teamwork. Natural way to enter conversation.

Examples 🔊

🏠 Life "Building on what you just said about the trip, I found this amazing hotel that's actually cheaper."
🏠 Life "Building on what Sarah mentioned about the restaurant, I know this place that has better reviews."
💼 Business "Building on what Minh just said about the timeline, I think we should also consider resource constraints."
💼 Business "Building on Sarah's point about customer feedback, we've actually seen similar patterns in our data."
Alternatives
  • "To add to what [Name] said..." (slightly more casual)
  • "Expanding on [Name]'s point..." (more formal)
  • "That's a great point, and I'd add..." (acknowledge + add)
💡 Usage Notes: Must reference what they actually said. Shows you're listening. Creates natural flow.
2
"Can I jump in here?"
/kæn aɪ dʒʌmp ɪn hɪər/
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High
Casual → Professional
Request permission to interject. Polite way to claim space when conversation is flowing fast.
Why C1?

"Can I" = respectful request. More confident than "Sorry to interrupt." Shows awareness of flow while claiming space.

Examples 🔊

🏠 Life "Can I jump in here? I actually know a great restaurant for that — my cousin works there."
🏠 Life "Can I jump in? I have a suggestion about the venue that might work better."
💼 Business "Can I jump in here? I have some data from last quarter that might be relevant to this discussion."
💼 Business "Can I jump in quickly? There's a compliance issue we should flag before we proceed."
Alternatives
  • "Mind if I jump in?" (more casual)
  • "Can I add something here?" (softer)
  • "If I could just jump in for a moment..." (more formal)
💡 Usage Notes: Wait for natural pause. Don't interrupt mid-sentence. Add value immediately after.
3
"I'd like to add something..."
/aɪd laɪk tuː æd ˈsʌmθɪŋ/
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High
Casual → Professional
Signal intention to contribute. Professional way to claim space without being aggressive.
Why C1?

"I'd like to" = confident but polite. States intention clearly. More professional than "Can I say something?"

Examples 🔊

🏠 Life "I'd like to add something about the budget — we should also consider hidden costs like maintenance."
🏠 Life "I'd like to add something from my experience with this — it's not as simple as it looks."
💼 Business "I'd like to add something from the client's perspective on this proposal — they're particularly concerned about timeline."
💼 Business "I'd like to add something about the risk assessment we conducted last quarter — it might change our approach."
Alternatives
  • "I'd just like to add..." (softer)
  • "If I may add..." (more formal)
  • "I just wanted to mention..." (more casual)
💡 Usage Notes: Must add NEW perspective, not repeat what was said. Shows you're contributing value.
4
"One thing I'd add..."
/wʌn θɪŋ aɪd æd/
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High
Casual → Professional
Add brief insight without taking over. Perfect for casual interjection when you have something short but valuable.
Why C1?

"One thing I'd add" = signal brevity. Shows respect for others' time. Higher frequency than "If I could just weigh in" in real life.

Examples 🔊

🏠 Life "One thing I'd add — I heard that restaurant has amazing dessert."
🏠 Life "One thing I'd add — this store has a better return policy if you're not sure."
💼 Business "One thing I'd like to add — we should also consider the budget implications before we commit."
💼 Business "One thing I'd add — our clients typically see results within 30 days, not 60."
Alternatives
  • "Oh, one thing..." (very casual)
  • "Just one quick point..." (brief)
  • "If I could just add one thing..." (more formal)
💡 Usage Notes: Must be truly brief (1-2 sentences). Violates promise if you talk for 5 minutes after saying "one thing."
5
"Just one quick thought..."
/dʒʌst wʌn kwɪk θɔːt/
⭐⭐⭐⭐ High
Casual → Professional
Add brief insight without taking over. Perfect for casual interjection when you have something short but valuable.
Why C1?

"Just one quick thought" = signal brevity. Shows respect for others' time. Perfect for casual interjection without dominating.

Examples 🔊

🏠 Life "Just one quick thought — what if we tested this with a small pilot first?"
🏠 Life "Just one quick thought — we should book the flights soon before prices go up."
💼 Business "Just one quick thought — what if we tested this with a small pilot before rolling out company-wide?"
💼 Business "Just one quick thought — our clients typically see results within 30 days, not 60."
Alternatives
  • "Oh, one thing..." (very casual)
  • "Just one quick point..." (brief)
  • "If I could just add one thing..." (more formal)
💡 Usage Notes: Must be truly brief. Emphasis on "quick" and "thought". Shows respect for time.

4 Mini Dialogues

Xem cách 5 interjection patterns được dùng trong thực tế. Click 🔊 để nghe.

💼 Dialogue 1: Team Meeting — Strategy Discussion

Context: Weekly team meeting. Manager discussing Q4 priorities. Sarah just proposed focusing on customer acquisition. You have data showing retention is actually the bigger opportunity.
"Sarah: I think we should focus on customer acquisition in Q4 to hit our growth targets.

You: Building on what Sarah just said about customer acquisition, I completely agree that's important. I'd like to add something from the analytics perspective — our data shows that retention is actually the bigger opportunity right now. We're losing 15% of customers each quarter.

Sarah: That's interesting. What does that mean for our strategy?

You: It means we might want to balance acquisition with retention efforts. Maybe 60% acquisition, 40% retention?"

Analysis — Why this works:

  • "Building on what Sarah just said" → Acknowledges her idea first, shows you're listening and building, not competing
  • "I completely agree that's important" → Validates her point before adding your perspective
  • "I'd like to add something" → Signals intention to contribute new perspective (not repeat)
  • "from the analytics perspective" → Shows you're adding unique value (data)
  • "We're losing 15% of customers" → Specific data point, not vague "retention is important"
  • 4-turn dialogue → Natural flow: Sarah proposes → You build → Sarah asks → You respond

🏠 Dialogue 2: Restaurant Misunderstanding — Unexpected Situation

Context: You're at a restaurant with friends. Waiter brings the wrong dish (extra onions when you asked for no onions). You need to correct them politely without making a scene.
"Waiter: Here's your pasta with extra onions, just like you ordered.

You: Can I jump in here? I think there might be a small misunderstanding — I actually asked for no onions.

Waiter: Oh, I'm sorry. Let me check the order again.

You: One thing I'd add — could you also check if it's the vegetarian version? I want to make sure there's no meat in the sauce.

Waiter: Of course, let me confirm with the kitchen.

Friend: No worries, these things happen.

You: Thanks for checking. I appreciate it."

Analysis — Why this works:

  • "Can I jump in here?" → Polite request to interject (unexpected situation)
  • "I think there might be a small misunderstanding" → Softens correction, not confrontational
  • "I actually asked for no onions" → Clear correction with specific detail
  • "One thing I'd add" → Adds second concern (vegetarian) briefly
  • 6-turn dialogue → Waiter serves → You correct → Waiter responds → You add → Waiter confirms → Friend comments → You thank
  • Graceful handling → Polite but clear, no scene, no embarrassment

💼 Dialogue 3: Client Presentation Q&A — Professional Challenge

Context: You're presenting to a client. They ask a challenging question about ROI. You need to answer professionally while adding valuable insights.
"Client: Can you elaborate on the expected ROI for this solution?

You: Building on your question about ROI, I'd like to share some specific numbers from similar implementations. One thing I'd like to add — our clients typically see 30% improvement in the first quarter, with full ROI within 12 months.

Client: What about the implementation costs?

You: From my perspective, the upfront investment is offset by the efficiency gains. Let me show you a case study..."

Analysis — Why this works:

  • "Building on your question" → Acknowledges client's question, shows you're addressing it directly
  • "I'd like to share some specific numbers" → Signals you're adding value (data)
  • "One thing I'd like to add" → Adds specific metric (30% improvement)
  • "30% improvement in the first quarter" → Specific, not vague "good results"
  • 4-turn dialogue → Client asks → You answer with data → Client asks follow-up → You provide insight
  • Professional tone → Confident, data-driven, solution-oriented

🏠 Dialogue 4: Doctor's Office — Asking for Clarification

Context: You're at the doctor's office. The doctor is explaining medication instructions too quickly. You need to ask them to slow down and clarify without seeming difficult.
"Doctor: So you'll take this twice daily with food, and watch for these side effects: nausea, dizziness, and potential interactions with other medications.

You: Can I jump in for a second? Could you slow down a bit? I want to make sure I understand the instructions correctly.

Doctor: Of course. Let me go over it again more slowly.

You: I'd just like to add that I'm also taking a blood pressure medication. Are there any interactions I should be aware of?

Doctor: Good question. Let me check that for you.

You: Thanks. I just want to make sure I'm taking everything safely."

Analysis — Why this works:

  • "Can I jump in for a second?" → Polite request (doctor speaking too fast)
  • "Could you slow down a bit?" → Clear request, not demanding
  • "I want to make sure I understand" → Shows you care about getting it right
  • "I'd just like to add" → Adds important medical info (blood pressure medication)
  • "Are there any interactions?" → Specific question, shows engagement
  • 6-turn dialogue → Doctor explains → You request → Doctor slows → You add info → Doctor responds → You thank
  • Respectful but clear → Not difficult, just careful about health

🎵 Intonation Patterns — When & Why

"Building on what [Name] just said..." ↘ Falling — Confident, collaborative. Dùng khi acknowledge + add value. Falling tone thể hiện bạn đang build, không compete. Cho thấy bạn listened và adding value.
💡 Tip: Pause nhẹ sau "said" trước khi add your point. Tạo natural flow.
"Can I jump in here?" ↗ Rising — Polite request. Rising tone shows respect. Cho thấy bạn aware of flow và asking permission.
💡 Tip: Wait for natural pause. Don't interrupt mid-sentence. Rising tone = question, not demand.
"I'd like to add something..." ↘ Falling — Confident contribution. Falling tone thể hiện bạn stating intention clearly. Professional, not aggressive.
💡 Tip: Emphasis on "add". Shows you're contributing value, not repeating.
"One thing I'd add..." ↘ Falling — Brief, confident. Falling tone + quick delivery. Shows respect for time.
💡 Tip: Must be truly brief (1-2 sentences). Violates promise if you talk for 5 minutes.
"Just one quick thought..." ↘ Falling — Brief, confident. Emphasis on "quick" and "thought". Fast delivery shows respect for time.
💡 Tip: Quick delivery is key. Shows you're not going to dominate.

Pro Tips

Build, don't bulldoze: Always acknowledge before adding your point.
Wait for natural pause: Don't interrupt mid-sentence.
Add value immediately: After "Can I jump in?", add clear, relevant value.
Be truly brief: "One thing I'd add" means 1-2 sentences, not 5 minutes.
Contribute strategically: Quality over quantity. One good point beats five random ones.
