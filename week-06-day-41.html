Today's Philosophy

From Agreement to Commitment

An agreement is when people say "yes." A commitment is when people know exactly what they're saying yes to.

Most meetings end with vague enthusiasm: "Great discussion! Let's make it happen!" Then nothing happens because no one knows who's doing what. C1 communicators don't leave meetings with agreements. They leave with commitments — specific, documented, and scheduled.

Important: Commitment isn't about control. It's about clarity. People don't resist commitments. They resist ambiguity. When you make commitments specific, you're not micromanaging — you're making it easy for everyone to follow through.

Common Mistakes

3 lỗi phổ biến khi locking in commitments. Tránh những lỗi này để giữ follow-through authentic.

Mistake 1: Leaving with vague "we'll figure it out"

❌ "Great discussion! Let's make it happen!" (rồi không ai làm gì)
✅ "Based on what we've discussed, you'll handle X by Friday, and I'll deliver Y by Wednesday. Does that capture it?"
Vague agreements feel good in the moment but accomplish nothing.

Mistake 2: Assuming everyone understood the same thing

❌ "We're all on the same page, right?" (rồi mỗi người hiểu khác nhau)
✅ "Let me confirm — you'll handle X, and I'll take care of Y. Does that match your understanding?"
People nod in meetings but leave with different interpretations. Confirmation prevents misalignment.

Mistake 3: Not following up after the meeting

❌ Send commitments and never check in
✅ Send summary email within 24 hours + schedule check-in meeting
Commitments without follow-up become forgotten promises.

Commitment Signals (Signature Section)

Strong vs Weak commitment language. Learn to recognize and use strong commitment signals.

📊 Commitment Signals

❌ Weak Commitment Signals (Avoid)

  • "I'll try."
  • "It should be okay."
  • "Hopefully."
  • "We'll see."
  • "I'll do my best."
  • "Let me see what I can do."
  • "I'll get to it when I can."

✅ Strong Commitment Signals (Use)

  • "I'll have it done by Friday."
  • "I'll send you an update Thursday."
  • "If anything changes, I'll let you know immediately."
  • "I'll deliver X by [date]."
  • "I'll take ownership of this."
  • "You can count on me for this."
  • "I'll follow up by [date] with [specific deliverable]."

💡 Why this matters: Weak commitment language creates ambiguity. Strong commitment language creates accountability. When you hear "I'll try," you should clarify: "Can you commit to a specific date?"

Reading Box: The Meeting That Accomplished Nothing

Read this meeting recap email. What's missing? (Hint: It has owners and deadlines, but something crucial is missing.)

📧 Meeting Recap Email

Subject: Meeting Recap - Q3 Planning

Hi team,

Great discussion today! Here's what we decided:

- Sarah will draft the customer retention plan by Friday
- Mike will redesign the onboarding process by next Wednesday
- Marketing and Sales will hold a joint planning session on Tuesday at 2 PM

Let's keep the momentum going!

Best,
Alex

🤔 Questions:

1. This email has owners and deadlines. So what's missing?

A. Enthusiasm | B. Success metrics and a check-in date | C. Owners | D. Deadlines

2. How would you rewrite it to create complete commitments?

A. "Hi team, Based on our discussion, here are the specific commitments: Sarah will draft the customer retention plan by Friday. Success metric: 10% improvement in retention by end of Q3. Mike will redesign the onboarding process by next Wednesday. Success metric: 20% faster onboarding time. Marketing and Sales will hold a joint planning session on Tuesday at 2 PM. Success metric: 3 joint campaigns launched by end of Q3. We'll review progress on all three items in our next meeting on [date]. Does this capture everyone's understanding?"

✅ Answers: 1. B. The email has owners and deadlines, but no success metrics and no check-in date. How will we know if Sarah's plan is successful? When will we review progress? Without these, commitments can drift. 2. A. The rewrite adds specific success metrics and a check-in date. Everyone knows exactly what they're committing to and how success will be measured.

🏠 Dialogue 1: Planning a Home Renovation (Life)

Context: You and your spouse just discussed renovating the kitchen. Need to lock in specific commitments before ending the conversation. Spouse has 1 light resistance about timeline.
You:
"Okay, so here's the plan — we're going to renovate the kitchen." Summarize
Spouse:
"Great! I'm excited."
You:
"Would you be comfortable handling the contractor research? I'd like to take care of the design and materials." Confirm Willingness + Assign
Spouse:
"Yeah, I can do that. When do we start?"
You:
"Let's commit to having 3 contractor quotes by next Friday. I'll have the design finalized by next Wednesday." Set Timeline
Spouse:
"Friday? That's a bit tight with work. Can we do Monday?" ← Light resistance
You:
"I understand. What if we commit to 2 quotes by Friday and 1 by Monday? Does that work?" Negotiate Timeline
Spouse:
"Yeah, that's more realistic."
You:
"How will we know this is successful? We'll have a clear timeline, fixed budget, and 5-star reviews from past clients." Define Success
Spouse:
"Perfect. What if we get stuck?"
You:
"Let's touch base on Sunday evening to review the quotes and make a decision. Does that work?" Schedule Check-in
Spouse:
"Yeah, Sunday at 7 PM?"
You:
"Perfect. Let me confirm — you'll get 2 quotes by Friday and 1 by Monday, I'll finalize the design by Wednesday, and we'll decide together on Sunday. Does that capture it?" Confirm Understanding
Spouse:
"Yep, that's it!"
You:
"Great. I'll send a quick text summary so we don't forget. Are we aligned on starting the renovation next month?" Final Confirmation
Spouse:
"Absolutely!"

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "So here's the plan..." → Summarize the Decision (Move 1)
  • "Would you be comfortable handling... I'd like to take care of..." → Confirm Willingness + Assign Ownership (Move 2)
  • "Let's commit to... by next Friday" → Set Timeline (Move 3)
  • Spouse said "Friday? That's a bit tight" → Negotiate Timeline (1 light resistance, not overwhelming)
  • "How will we know this is successful?" → Define Success Metrics (Move 4)
  • "Let's touch base on Sunday evening" → Schedule Check-in (Move 5)
  • Register: Casual throughout (spouses)
  • 14 turns natural flow, all 5 moves used naturally
  • 1 light resistance about timeline (realistic, shows commitment can be negotiated)
  • Ends with confirmation to ensure alignment

🎵 Intonation Patterns — When & Why

"Based on what we've discussed..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when summarizing decision. Falling tone = confident, authoritative. Avoid: Rising (sounds like you're questioning).
"Would you be comfortable..." ↗ Rising Context: Used when confirming willingness. Rising tone = genuine question, seeking input. Avoid: Falling (sounds like you already know).
"Let's commit to..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when setting timeline. Falling tone = decisive, serious. Avoid: Rising (sounds uncertain).

1. (Life) You and your spouse just agreed to renovate the kitchen. Best first move?

A. "Great, let's do it!"
B. "So here's the plan — we're renovating the kitchen."
C. "You do X, I'll do Y."
D. "When do we start?"

2. (Business) You just finished a team meeting. Best first move?

A. "Great meeting everyone!"
B. "Based on what we've discussed, here's what we've decided..."
C. "Let's commit to deadlines."
D. "Who's doing what?"

3. (Judgement) Colleague says "I'll try my best to get it done." This is:

A. Specific commitment
B. Vague agreement
C. Clear deadline
D. Assigned ownership

4. (Judgement) Colleague says "I'll get back to you." Best response?

A. "Okay, thanks."
B. "By when? With what information?"
C. "No problem."
D. "Let's keep the momentum going."

5. (Life) You and your spouse confirmed willingness. Next move?

A. Assign ownership
B. Set timeline
C. Define success metrics
D. Summarize decision

6. (Business) You set the timeline. Next move?

A. Assign ownership
B. Define success metrics
C. Schedule check-in
D. Summarize decision

7. (Judgement) Meeting ends with "Great discussion! Let's make it happen!" What's missing?

A. Enthusiasm
B. Specific commitments with owners, deadlines, success metrics, and check-in date
C. Agreement
D. Action

8. (Business) Client says "We're aligned." Best response?

A. "Great!"
B. "Let me confirm — you'll handle X by Friday, and I'll deliver Y by Wednesday. Does that capture it?"
C. "We're on the same page."
D. "Let's keep the momentum going."

9. (Life) You locked in commitments with your spouse. Best final move?

A. Say "Great!"
B. Send a quick text summary so you don't forget
C. Forget about it
D. Assume they'll remember

Reality Check

Commitment isn't about control. It's about clarity.

People don't resist commitments. They resist ambiguity. When you make commitments specific, you're not micromanaging — you're making it easy for everyone to follow through.

Key insight: The goal isn't to create paperwork. The goal is to ensure everyone leaves the meeting knowing exactly what they're committing to, by when, and how success will be measured.
