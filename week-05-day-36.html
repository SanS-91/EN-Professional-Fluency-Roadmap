Today's Philosophy

Understand Before Persuading

Great negotiators don't solve positions first. They uncover the reason behind them.

4 Principles of Week 6: Understand before persuading • Interests before positions • Value before compromise • Commitment before agreement.

Today is about Move 1: Uncovering interests. No solutions yet. Just deep understanding.

Why most negotiations fail
  • Argue positions instead of uncovering interests
  • Listen to respond instead of listen to understand
  • Compromise too early instead of creating value
What C1 negotiators do differently
  • Ask "Tell me more about..." before proposing solutions
  • Dig deeper: "What's most important to you about this?"
  • Create options: "What if we structured it this way..."

Interest Decoder

Integrated Dialogues

See how C1 speakers uncover interests in real conversations. No labels in real life — just natural flow.

Dialogue 1: Vacation Planning Conflict (Life)

Context: You and your spouse are planning a vacation. You want beach, they want mountains. Budget is $5K.
You:
"I love both ideas. What's making the mountains important to you?" Clarify
Spouse:
"I just need to get away from the city. I'm burnt out and need nature."
You:
"That makes sense. What's most important to you — the scenery, the activities, or just being somewhere quiet?" Prioritize
Spouse:
"Honestly, just quiet. No crowds, no noise."
You:
"You've got a point there. What if we found a beach house that's off the main tourist area? We'd get the ocean, but also the quiet you need."
Spouse:
"That could work. As long as it's not crowded."
You:
"So if I'm hearing you correctly, the goal is minimal people, not total isolation?" Check
Spouse:
"Exactly. I don't need to be alone, just not in a tourist trap."
You:
"Got it. Would it be fair to say the real challenge is finding the right location within budget?" Reframe
Spouse:
"Both, honestly. I want somewhere nice but not $10K nice."
You:
"If we could solve the location issue with a secluded beach rental under $5K, would that address your concern?" Test
Spouse:
"That would be perfect. Can you find some options?"
You:
"Absolutely. Next steps are: I'll research 3 beach options and 3 mountain options by tonight. We'll compare them together tomorrow. Does that timeline work?"
Spouse:
"Perfect. Thanks for listening."

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "What's making that important?" → Clarify (Move 1)
  • "What's most important to you?" → Prioritize (Move 2)
  • "So if I'm hearing you correctly..." → Check (Move 3)
  • "Would it be fair to say..." → Reframe (Move 4)
  • "If we could solve X, would that address your concern?" → Test (Move 5)
  • Register: Casual throughout (spouses)
  • 14 turns natural flow, uncovering interests instead of arguing positions

💼 Dialogue 2: Budget Negotiation (Business - Interest Discovery Only)

Context: You're discussing a budget increase with your manager. They are resistant. Day 1 of Week 6 - focus on interest discovery only. Using only 3 core moves: Clarify, Prioritize, Check. No solutions yet.
Manager:
"I've reviewed your budget request. We can't approve the full amount. The company is focused on cost control right now."
You:
"I understand the focus on cost control. Tell me more about what's driving that concern — is it about total spend this quarter, or is there a specific budget line that's under pressure?" Clarify
Manager:
"It's both. We're trying to keep Q4 expenses flat, and your project is the largest line item."
You:
"That makes sense. What's most important to you about keeping expenses flat — is it hitting quarterly targets, or something else?" Prioritize
Manager:
"Mainly hitting targets. If we go over, it affects our bonus structure."
You:
"So if I'm hearing you correctly, the concern is less about the project itself, and more about how it impacts the team's quarterly performance?" Check
Manager:
"Exactly. I'm not saying no to the project. I'm saying we need to be careful about timing."
You:
"It sounds like the real concern is cash flow timing, not the project's value?" Check
Manager:
"Yes, that's it. If we could spread it out, that would help."
You:
"That's really helpful to understand. Thanks for being transparent about this."
Manager:
"Of course. I want to make sure we're aligned."
You:
"Absolutely. Let me think about a few options and come back to you tomorrow with some ideas on how we might structure this."
Manager:
"Sounds good. I'll look forward to it."

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "Tell me more about what's driving..." → Clarify (Move 1)
  • "What's most important to you about..." → Prioritize (Move 2)
  • "So if I'm hearing you correctly..." / "It sounds like..." → Check (Move 3)
  • Register: Professional (manager-employee)
  • 12 turns, stops at interest discovery (no solution proposed yet)
  • Day 1 of Week 6 - focus on uncovering interests. Day 37 will develop the business case.
  • Only 3 moves used - Reframe and Test saved for Practice scenarios

🎵 Intonation Patterns — When & Why

"Tell me more about..." ↗ Rising Context: Used when curious. Rising tone = open, non-threatening. Avoid: Falling (sounds like a demand).
"What's most important..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when prioritizing. Falling tone = focused, serious. Avoid: Rising (sounds uncertain).
"It sounds like..." ↗↘ Rise-fall Context: Used when paraphrasing. Rise-fall = checking understanding, not assuming. Avoid: Flat (sounds robotic).

Speaking: 4 Scenarios

Practice uncovering interests in different contexts. Remember: no register is "better" — it's about appropriateness!

🟢 Casual - 3-4 min

Scenario 1: Changing Plans with Friend

Friend wants to change meeting from Saturday to Sunday. You don't like Sundays.

💡 Model Opening: "What's making Sunday important for you?" Possible Response: "I hear you — Sunday works better for your schedule. What's making Sunday important — is it a family thing, or just timing? What's most important to you about meeting this weekend? If we could find a time that works for both of us, would that address your concern?"

Requirements:

  • Use "What's making Sunday important?"
  • Use "What would success look like?"
  • Use "If we could solve X, would that work?"
  • Keep it collaborative, not argumentative
🔵 Neutral - 3-4 min

Scenario 2: Colleague's Deadline Push

Colleague wants data 2 days earlier. You can't make it.

💡 Model Opening: "Can you walk me through what's driving that deadline?" Possible Response: "I understand you need this sooner. Can you walk me through what's driving that deadline — is there a meeting or decision depending on it? What's most important to you about getting it by Wednesday? So if I'm hearing you correctly, the concern is about having the data for the Thursday presentation?"

Requirements:

  • Use "Tell me more about what's driving that deadline"
  • Use "What's the real challenge here?"
  • Use "If we could solve X, would that address your concern?"
  • Uncover interest, not argue position
🟣 Professional - 4-5 min

Scenario 3: Vendor Price Negotiation

Vendor says price is non-negotiable. You need to reduce it.

💡 Model Opening: "Help me understand what's driving the current pricing." 📖 Possible Response: "I appreciate you being upfront about the pricing. Help me understand what's driving the current price point — is it about maintaining margins, or is there a volume threshold we could hit? What's most important to you about keeping the price at this level? It sounds like the concern is about setting a precedent for other clients?"

Requirements:

  • Use "Tell me more about what's driving the price point"
  • Use "What's most important to you about maintaining this price?"
  • Use "If we could solve X, would that address your concern?"
  • Create value, not just negotiate price
🟠 Challenge - 5 min

Scenario 4: The Double Negotiation

Morning: Negotiate house purchase with spouse (Casual). Afternoon: Negotiate workload with manager (Professional).

💡 Model Opening (Life): "What's making this house important to you?" Model Opening (Business): "Tell me more about what's driving the workload concern." 📖 Possible Response (Life): "I love that you're thinking about our future. What's making this house important to you right now — is it the location, the size, or something else? What's most important to you about making this move? So if I'm hearing you correctly, the concern is about having enough space for the kids?"

Requirements:

  • Shift register flexibly
  • Use different moves for each context

  • Uncover interests, not argue positions

🎯 Negotiation Mindset Mastery

Communication Outcomes

You ask more questions than you make statements
The other person feels understood
You uncover the real issue, not the surface problem
Solutions address interests, not positions
Both parties feel they've gained value

Pro Tips

Listen 70%, talk 30%: Negotiation is about understanding, not persuading
Vary your openings: Don't just say "Help me understand..." — use "Tell me more...", "Walk me through...", "What's making that important?"
Dig deeper: First answer is usually the position, not the interest
Test assumptions: "If we could solve X, would that address your concern?"
Create before dividing: Make the pie bigger before you slice it
