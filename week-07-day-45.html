Today's Philosophy

From Hearing to Listening

Hearing is passive (sound waves hit your ears). Listening is active (you decode meaning, emotion, and the unsaid).

Most leaders think their job is to have all the answers. C1 leaders know their job is to ask the right questions. When you listen deeply, people feel seen. When you ask powerful questions, people discover their own answers.

Important: Powerful questions are not interrogation questions. Interrogation questions close people down ("Why did you do that?"). Powerful questions open people up ("What were you hoping would happen?").

Common Mistakes

3 common mistakes when listening. Avoid these to keep conversations deep and authentic.

Mistake 1: Listening to respond instead of to understand

❌ Nodding while mentally preparing your reply.
✅ Fully present, letting their words sink in before you speak.
When you listen to respond, you miss 50% of what they're saying.

Mistake 2: Asking "Why" questions that put people on defense

❌ "Why did you make that decision?" (Sounds judgmental)
✅ "What led you to that decision?" (Sounds curious)
"Why" triggers justification. "What led you to" invites explanation.

Mistake 3: Filling every silence with advice

❌ Jumping in with solutions before they finish processing.
✅ Holding silence for 3-5 seconds after they speak. Let them go deeper.
Silence is where insight happens. Your advice often interrupts their own discovery.

Reading Box: The Email That Wasn't Really About the Deadline

Read this email from an employee. Pause and analyze what's REALLY being said before reading the model answer.

📧 The Deadline Email

Subject: Quick question about the project timeline

Hi [Manager],

I wanted to check if we can push the Friday deadline to next Wednesday. I know this is inconvenient, but I've been working on three other projects this week and I'm a bit overwhelmed.

I'll make sure to get it done as soon as possible. Sorry for the inconvenience.

Thanks,
Alex

🤔 Your Turn: Pause and Analyze

Before reading the answer, ask yourself:

1. What is Alex REALLY saying beneath the surface?
2. What would a B2 manager reply? What would a C1 manager reply?

✅ Model Answer:

1. The Unsaid: Alex is not just asking for a deadline extension. Alex is signaling burnout, lack of prioritization support, and possibly feeling undervalued. The "sorry for the inconvenience" is a red flag — Alex feels guilty for having limits.
2. B2 Reply: "Sure, Wednesday works. Just get it done." (Misses the real issue; Alex stays burnt out).
3. C1 Reply: "Thanks for flagging this, Alex. Before we adjust the deadline, I want to check in — you mentioned three other projects. What's the real challenge here? Is it workload, prioritization, or something else? Let's talk for 15 minutes today." (Listens for the unsaid, asks powerful questions, creates space).

Micro Writing: Rewrite Ordinary Questions to Powerful Questions

Rewrite these ordinary questions into powerful questions. Explain WHY your version is better.

✍️ Ordinary → Powerful Questions

❌ Ordinary (Business): "Why did you miss the deadline?"
Weak"What happened with the deadline?"
Better"What got in the way of the deadline?"
Powerful"What were you hoping would happen with the timeline, and what got in the way?"
WHY: "Why" triggers defensiveness. The powerful version assumes positive intent and invites reflection on both the goal and the obstacle.
❌ Ordinary (Life): "Why are you so upset?"
Weak"What's making you upset?"
Better"What's bothering you right now?"
Powerful"What's the thing that matters most to you right now?"
WHY: "Why are you upset" sounds dismissive of their emotion. The powerful version honors their feelings and helps them identify the core value that's been threatened.

🎧 Listening Comprehension

1. Which sentence made the employee feel safe enough to share their real fear?

✅ "I appreciate you sharing that. Help me understand what's really going on here." (It showed curiosity, not judgment, and invited depth).

2. Why did the leader pause for 3 seconds after "people getting promoted who haven't been here as long"?

Grammar in Context

Why we use these specific structures when listening deeply.

📝 Grammar for Deep Listening

1. Indirect Questions for Softening ("I'm wondering what's behind that...") We use "I'm wondering..." or "I'm curious about..." instead of direct questions. It creates psychological safety and sounds like curiosity, not interrogation.
2. Reflective Statements ("What I'm hearing is...") We use "What I'm hearing is..." or "If I understand correctly..." to confirm understanding without imposing our own interpretation. It invites correction if we're wrong.
3. Open-ended vs Closed Questions "What would success look like?" (Open - invites exploration) vs "Do you want to quit?" (Closed - forces a yes/no). Open questions unlock discovery.

🎵 Intonation Patterns — When & Why

"What's behind that?" ↗ Rising Context: Used when listening for the unsaid. Rising tone = inviting, curious. Avoid: Falling (sounds like an accusation).
"So what matters most to you is..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when summarizing. Falling tone = grounded, confirming. Avoid: Rising (sounds like you're unsure of your summary).
"What would success look like?" ↗ Rising then ↘ Falling Context: Used when asking powerful questions. Rise on "What", fall on "like". Avoid: Flat (sounds like a survey question).

Speaking: 4 Scenarios

Practice deep listening in different contexts. Focus on the communication goal, not just using moves.

🟢 Casual - 3-4 min

Scenario 1: Listening to a Friend in Crisis

Your friend just went through a divorce. They say "I'm fine" but their voice and eyes say otherwise. Listen for what's NOT being said.

💡 Communication Goal: Listen for the unsaid & hold space. 📖 Possible Response: "You say you're okay, but something feels off. There's something more, isn't there? ... (Pause 3 seconds) ... I'm here. What's really going on?"

Requirements:

  • Notice the mismatch between words and emotion
  • Ask gently what's behind it
  • Hold silence to let them open up
  • Reflect back what you hear
🔵 Neutral - 3-4 min

Scenario 2: Listening to an Employee Who Wants to Quit

A high-performing employee says they want to leave. Don't try to keep them immediately. Listen for the real reason.

💡 Communication Goal: Reflect back & ask powerful questions. 📖 Possible Response: "I appreciate you telling me. Help me understand what's really going on here. ... If I understand correctly, the core issue is growth, not the work itself. Is that right? ... What would success look like for you in the next year?"

Requirements:

  • Acknowledge their courage to share
  • Ask what's really going on
  • Reflect back their core issue
  • Ask a powerful question about their future
🟣 Professional - 4-5 min

Scenario 3: Listening in a Team Conflict

Two team members are arguing fiercely about a project approach. You need to listen to both to find the real fear behind the conflict.

💡 Communication Goal: Summarize for clarity & ask powerful questions. 📖 Possible Response: "Let me make sure I'm hearing both of you correctly. Sarah, you're worried about speed. Mike, you're worried about quality. Is that right? ... What would success look like for both of you? ... What matters most to each of you here?"

Requirements:

  • Reflect back both perspectives accurately
  • Find the underlying values (speed vs quality)
  • Ask powerful questions to shift from positions to interests
  • Summarize the shared goal
🟠 Challenge - 5 min

Scenario 4: The Double Listening Challenge

Morning: Listen to your child talk about school pressure (Casual). Afternoon: Listen to a colleague talk about burnout (Professional).

💡 Communication Goal: Shift register. Life = emotional safety. Business = growth & ownership. 📖 Possible Response (Life): "You say school is fine, but something feels off. What's really going on? ... (Pause) ... So what matters most to you right now is feeling capable, not just getting good grades. Is that right?" 📖 Possible Response (Business): "I appreciate you sharing this. Help me understand what's really going on here. ... If I understand correctly, the core issue is workload, not the work itself. ... What would success look like for you in terms of balance?"

Requirements:

  • Shift register flexibly
  • Life = emotional safety, validation. Business = growth, ownership, balance.
  • Listen for the unsaid in both
  • Ask powerful questions in both
