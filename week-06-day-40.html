Today's Philosophy

From Persuasion to Influence

People rarely support ideas. People support ideas they helped shape.

Influence isn't about convincing people to agree with you. It's about bringing people into the process so the idea becomes partially theirs. When you build influence correctly, by the time you reach the decision-maker, the answer is often already clear.

Important: Influence doesn't mean everyone agrees. It means enough people trust the process to move forward together. Someone can support you without fully agreeing. Building influence doesn't mean everyone says yes — it means enough people say "I can work with this."

Common Mistakes

3 lỗi phổ biến khi building influence. Tránh những lỗi này để giữ collaboration authentic.

Mistake 1: Asking for support before building input

❌ "Can you support my proposal?" (ngay lần đầu nói chuyện)
✅ "I'm exploring something and would value your perspective..." (build first)
People support what they helped shape, not what they were told to support.

Mistake 2: Collecting supporters instead of building contributors

❌ "I need 5 people to agree with me." (collecting signatures)
✅ "I need 5 people to help shape this." (building contributors)
Supporters can disappear. Contributors stay invested because they helped create it.

Mistake 3: Going to the decision-maker too early

❌ Đi thẳng đến CEO mà không build influence
✅ Map stakeholders → Find allies → Build ownership → Present together
Decision-makers listen to coalitions, not individuals. Influence makes the decision obvious.

Ally vs Decision Maker Decoder (Signature Section)

Who should you talk to first? Understanding roles in influence building.

🔍 Ally vs Decision Maker Decoder

PersonRoleHow to approachWhen to approach
Decision makerFinal yes/noPresent with coalition already builtLast, after allies are on board
AllyHelps shape decisionSeek perspective and inputFirst, before anyone else
InfluencerTrusted voiceAsk for endorsementAfter ally, before decision-maker
BlockerNeeds reassuranceAddress concerns first, then inviteAfter understanding their worry

💡 How to use: 1. Map everyone involved in the decision. 2. Identify who is ally, influencer, blocker, decision-maker. 3. Start with allies (seek perspective). 4. Build ownership with influencers (invite contribution). 5. Address blockers' concerns (find shared interest). 6. Present to decision-maker with coalition already built.

📧 Email from Colleague

I've been working on a new process for handling client requests, and I think it will save us a lot of time. The director is reviewing proposals next week, and I'm planning to present mine.

Alex

🤔 Questions:

1. Alex didn't ask for support directly. So what's wrong?

A. The email is too short | B. Alex skipped ALL the influence-building moves — no curiosity, no shared interest, no invitation to contribute, no ownership building | C. Alex should have attached a document | D. Nothing is wrong

2. How would you rewrite this email to build influence before the presentation?

A. "Hi team, I'm exploring a new approach to handling client requests and would value your perspective before I take it further. I noticed this connects to the efficiency improvements you've been working on, and your input would really help shape this. Would you be open to being part of this? ✅ Answers: 1. B. Alex is presenting a finished idea instead of shaping it with the team. By the time Alex presents to the director, the team has no reason to support it. 2. A. The rewrite starts with curiosity, finds shared interest, invites contribution, and builds ownership — BEFORE asking anyone to present.

Integrated Dialogues

See how C1 speakers build influence in real conversations. No labels in real life — just natural flow.

🏠 Dialogue 1: Building Family Support for Career Change (Life)

Context: You want to change careers (from accounting to design). Instead of going straight to parents, you build influence with your brother first. Brother is initially skeptical.
You:
"Anh, em muốn nói với anh về một quyết định quan trọng. I'm exploring something and would value your perspective." Start with Curiosity
Brother:
"Sure, what's up?"
You:
"Em đang cân nhắc chuyển từ kế toán sang design. Em biết đây là quyết định lớn, nên em muốn nghe ý kiến anh trước khi nói với bố mẹ."
Brother:
"Hmm, that's a big change. Are you sure about this?"
You:
"I'm not 100% sure yet, which is why I want your perspective. I noticed this connects to what you did when you switched from engineering to product management." Find Shared Interest
Brother:
"Yeah, it was tough but worth it. But design is different."
You:
"That's true. Your input would really help shape this — you've been through a similar transition and know what questions Mom and Dad will ask." Invite Contribution
Brother:
"I guess I can help you think through it. What's your plan?"
You:
"Em đã research một số khóa học và cơ hội việc làm. Would you be open to being part of this? Maybe we could work on the presentation to Mom and Dad together?" Build Ownership
Brother:
"Okay, I can do that. But you need to have solid answers about job security."
You:
"Exactly. Would you be comfortable backing this when we talk to them? Your perspective carries weight with Mom." Ask for Visible Support
Brother:
"I can back you up. But let's refine the plan first."
You:
"Perfect. Cảm ơn anh. Em cũng sẽ nói chuyện với cô Lan. Your input would really help with the financial concerns."
Brother:
"Good idea. She can help with that part."
You:
"Nếu chúng ta cùng nhau, sẽ dễ hơn."
Brother:
"Đúng. Teamwork makes the dream work."

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "I'm exploring something and would value your perspective" → Start with Curiosity (Move 1)
  • "I noticed this connects to what you did..." → Find Shared Interest (Move 2)
  • "Your input would really help shape this..." → Invite Contribution (Move 3)
  • "Would you be open to being part of this?" → Build Ownership (Move 4)
  • "Would you be comfortable backing this..." → Ask for Visible Support (Move 5)
  • Register: Casual throughout (siblings)
  • 14 turns natural flow, all 5 moves used naturally
  • Brother was initially skeptical ("Are you sure?") — had to build influence, not just ask for support

💼 Dialogue 2: Building Cross-Functional Coalition (Business)

Context: You want to propose a new project management tool. Marketing Lead is initially not convinced because of the learning curve.
You:
"Hi Sarah, I'm exploring something and would value your perspective before I go further." Start with Curiosity
Marketing Lead:
"Sure, what's on your mind?"
You:
"I'm considering proposing a new project management tool for the team. I noticed this connects to what you've been working on — the workflow efficiency improvements." Find Shared Interest
Marketing Lead:
"Interesting. What tool are you thinking?"
You:
"Asana. But I'm not sure it's the right fit yet. Your input would really help shape this because you've led similar transitions before." Invite Contribution
Marketing Lead:
"I've used Asana. Honestly, I'm not convinced. The learning curve is steep and my team is already overloaded."
You:
"That's a valid concern. What if we looked at this differently? Instead of a full rollout, what if we started with a small pilot — just our two teams for 4 weeks?" Address concern + Expand options
Marketing Lead:
"A pilot might work. But I'd need to see it doesn't slow us down."
You:
"Would you be open to being part of this? We could design the pilot together, with your team's constraints in mind." Build Ownership
Marketing Lead:
"I'd be willing to try that. What about Engineering?"
You:
"I'm talking to Mike next. Would you be comfortable backing this when we present to the director? Your perspective carries weight, and having both Marketing and Engineering on board would make a stronger case." Ask for Visible Support
Marketing Lead:
"I can back you up if the pilot shows results. But you'll need data on ROI."
You:
"Exactly. If we can show efficiency gains from the pilot, it'll be easier to get approval."
Marketing Lead:
"Smart approach. Let's sync next week to plan the pilot."
You:
"Perfect. Thanks for being open to this."
Marketing Lead:
"No problem. This could really help both our teams — if we do it right."

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "I'm exploring something and would value your perspective..." → Start with Curiosity (Move 1)
  • "I noticed this connects to what you've been working on..." → Find Shared Interest (Move 2)
  • "Your input would really help shape this..." → Invite Contribution (Move 3)
  • Marketing Lead said "I'm not convinced" — had to address concern before continuing
  • "Would you be open to being part of this?" → Build Ownership (Move 4)
  • "Would you be comfortable backing this..." → Ask for Visible Support (Move 5)
  • Register: Professional (cross-functional leads)
  • 15 turns, complete influence building conversation
  • Marketing Lead was initially skeptical ("I'm not convinced") — had to address concern and expand options before building ownership

🎵 Intonation Patterns — When & Why

"I'm exploring something..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when starting with curiosity. Falling tone = sincere, open. Avoid: Rising (sounds like you're questioning).
"Your input would really help..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when inviting contribution. Falling tone = genuine, appreciative. Avoid: Rising (sounds like flattery).
"Would you be comfortable backing..." ↗↘ Rise-fall Context: Used when asking for visible support. Rise-fall = respectful question, not demand. Avoid: Flat (sounds robotic) or Falling (sounds like a demand).

Reality Check

Influence doesn't mean everyone agrees. It means enough people trust the process to move forward together.

Someone can support you without fully agreeing. Building influence doesn't mean everyone says yes — it means enough people say "I can work with this."

Key insight: The goal isn't unanimous agreement. The goal is enough support to make the decision obvious.
