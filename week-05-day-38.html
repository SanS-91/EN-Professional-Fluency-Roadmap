Week 6 không dạy persuasion. Week 6 dạy negotiation thinking. Hôm nay bạn sẽ học cách biến lo ngại thành cơ hội. B2 speakers see objections as attacks. C1 speakers see them as information.

Today's Philosophy

From Defensive to Curious

When someone raises a concern, your instinct is to defend or give in. C1 communicators do something different: they acknowledge, validate, and address. They turn pushback into progress.

Important: These 5 moves are communication tools, not mandatory steps. Choose the move that fits the concern. Sometimes you only need 2 moves. Sometimes you need all 5. Judgement matters more than following a process.

Common Mistakes

3 lỗi phổ biến khi handle concerns. Tránh những lỗi này để giữ conversation productive.

Mistake 1: Explain too early

❌ "No, that's not a problem. Let me explain why..."
✅ "I understand your concern about [X]. That's a valid point."
Acknowledge first, explain second. People need to feel heard before they can listen.

Mistake 2: Give data to emotions

❌ Spouse says "I'm scared" → You say "The data shows 85% success rate"
✅ "I get why you're scared. That's a big change. Let's take it step by step."
Match the concern type. Emotional concerns need empathy, not evidence.

Mistake 3: Use all 5 moves every time

❌ "I understand... That's valid... What if... The data shows... Here's how..."
✅ Choose the fewest moves needed. Quality over quantity.
Over-explaining sounds defensive. Sometimes 2 moves are enough.

📧 Email from Client

🤔 Questions:

1. What's the real concern?

A. Budget is too high | B. They need proof it will work | C. Timing is bad - they're too busy right now | D. All of the above, but C is the biggest blocker

2. What communication move would you use first? WHY?

A. Acknowledge (to show you heard them) | B. Provide Evidence (to address their concerns) | C. Offer Mitigation (to solve the timing issue) | D. Reframe (to change their perspective)

✅ Answers: 1. D, but C is the hidden blocker. The "busy period" is code for "we can't commit to this right now." 2. A. Acknowledge first. Show you heard all three concerns before addressing them. Best response: Acknowledge their busy period + offer to revisit after year-end.

Integrated Dialogues

See how C1 speakers handle concerns in real conversations. No labels in real life — just natural flow.

🏠 Dialogue 1: Partner Worried About Moving (Life)

Context: You and your spouse are discussing moving house. Spouse has many concerns. Note: Only 3-4 moves used - Acknowledge, Validate, Mitigation. No hard data with spouse!
You:
"I've been thinking more about the move. Can I walk you through what I've found?"
Spouse:
"I'm still not sure about this. What about the kids?"
You:
"I get why you're worried about the kids. That's really important." Acknowledge
Spouse:
"They'll lose all their friends. They're already settled here."
You:
"You're right to worry about that. It's a big transition for them." Validate
Spouse:
"And what about my job? I just got promoted."
You:
"That's a valid concern. I've already started looking into companies there. There seem to be quite a few opportunities in your field." Validate Reframe (soft)
Spouse:
"Really? Which ones?"
You:
"I found a few that match your profile. Remember when we moved before? We were worried about the same things, and it worked out much better than we expected." Evidence (experience)
Spouse:
"I don't know. It still feels risky."
You:
"Here's what we can do about that — we'll visit the new schools together, talk to other parents, and make sure it's a good fit before we commit. We can also time the move for after your bonus." Mitigation
Spouse:
"I guess that makes sense. But I'm still nervous."
You:
"I get why you're nervous. This is a big decision. What if we take it one step at a time — first we visit, then we decide. Are we aligned on that approach?" Acknowledge Mitigation
Spouse:
"Yeah, let's do that. One step at a time."

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "I get why you're worried about..." → Acknowledge (Move 1)
  • "You're right to worry about..." → Validate (Move 2)
  • "Remember when we moved before?" → Evidence (Move 4 - experience, not data!)
  • "Here's what we can do about that..." → Mitigation (Move 5)
  • Register: Casual throughout (spouses)
  • 12 turns natural flow, handling concerns with empathy
  • Only 4 moves used - no hard Reframe, no data

💼 Dialogue 2: Client Pushing Back on Timeline (Business)

Context: You're presenting a proposal to a client. Client has concerns about timeline and risk. Note: Uses all 5 moves naturally, with one extra pushback.
Client:
"I've reviewed your proposal. I like the approach, but I'm concerned about the 6-month timeline."
You:
"I appreciate you raising that concern. Timeline is critical for this project." Acknowledge
Client:
"We need results by Q3. Six months puts us in Q4."
You:
"That's a valid concern. Here's how I'd address it — we can phase the delivery to give you early wins in Q3 while completing the full project in Q4." Validate Mitigation
Client:
"What do you mean by 'early wins'?"
You:
"What if we approached it this way — we deliver the core functionality in 3 months, which gives you immediate value, then we add the advanced features in months 4-6." Reframe
Client:
"But what if the core functionality isn't enough?"
You:
"The data actually shows that 80% of the value comes from the core features. The advanced features are nice-to-haves, not must-haves." Evidence
Client:
"I'm still worried about the risk. What if something goes wrong?"
You:
"Here's how we'd mitigate that risk — we'll build in 2-week buffers at each phase, and we'll have weekly check-ins to catch issues early." Mitigation
Client:
"Okay, that makes sense. But I need to discuss this with my team."
You:
"I understand your concern about getting team buy-in. Here's what we can do — I'll prepare a summary document highlighting the key points, and I'm available to join your team meeting to answer questions." Acknowledge Mitigation
Client:
"That would be helpful. Let's schedule that for next week."
You:
"Perfect. Are we aligned on me sending the summary by Friday and joining your meeting next Wednesday?"
Client:
"Yes, let's do that."

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "I appreciate you raising that concern..." → Acknowledge (Move 1)
  • "That's a valid concern..." → Validate (Move 2)
  • "What if we approached it this way..." → Reframe (Move 3)
  • "The data actually shows..." → Evidence (Move 4)
  • "Here's how we'd mitigate that risk..." → Mitigation (Move 5)
  • Register: Professional (client-vendor)
  • 14 turns, complete concern-handling conversation
  • All 5 moves used naturally - because business concerns often need full range

🎵 Intonation Patterns — When & Why

"I understand your concern..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when acknowledging. Falling tone = sincere, empathetic. Avoid: Rising (sounds like you're questioning their concern).
"That's a valid point..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when validating. Falling tone = respectful, serious. Avoid: Too soft (làm mất sense of validation).
"What if we approached..." ↗ Rising Context: Used when reframing. Rising tone = inviting, curious. Avoid: Falling (sounds like you're telling them what to do).

Speaking: 4 Scenarios

Practice handling concerns in different contexts. Remember: no register is "better" — it's about appropriateness!

🟢 Casual - 3-4 min

Scenario 1: Friend Worried About Your Decision

You want to explain an important decision (career change, moving, big purchase) to a friend. They have concerns. Handle concerns with empathy.

💡 Model Opening: "I understand your concern about [X]. That's a valid point." 📖 Possible Response: "I get why you're worried about the career change. That's a big decision. You're right to think about the risks. Remember when I considered changing jobs 2 years ago? We talked about it and decided to wait. This feels different because I've already researched the new role. Here's what we can do — I'll take the new job on a 3-month trial, and if it doesn't work out, I have options." 🔄 Alternative Response: "I hear you. It's a big change. What if we looked at it this way — I'm not leaving the company, I'm growing my career. From what I've seen, people who make these moves usually end up happier. Let's take it step by step."

Requirements:

  • Use "I understand your concern about..."
  • Use experience/memory instead of data
  • Use "Here's what we can do..."
  • Keep it collaborative, not defensive
🔵 Neutral - 3-4 min

Scenario 2: Team Member Concerned About Change

You want to propose a change to the team (new process, new tool, new workflow). Team has concerns. Handle concerns calmly.

💡 Model Opening: "I appreciate you raising that concern. Let me address that." 📖 Possible Response: "I appreciate you raising that concern about the new tool. That's a valid point — learning something new takes time. Here's how we'd handle that — we'll do a 2-week pilot with just 2 people first, then roll out to the rest of the team. The data actually shows that teams using this tool complete projects 30% faster. What if we approached it this way — we start small, learn from it, then scale up?"

Requirements:

  • Use "That's a valid concern..."
  • Use "Here's how we'd handle that..."
  • Use "The data actually shows..."
  • Address concerns upfront
🟣 Professional - 4-5 min

Scenario 3: Manager Pushing Back on Proposal

You present a proposal to your manager. Manager has concerns about budget, timeline, or risk. Handle concerns professionally.

💡 Model Opening: "I understand your concern about [X]. That's a valid point." 📖 Possible Response: "I understand your concern about the budget. That's a valid point — it's a significant investment. What if we approached it this way — instead of seeing this as a cost, we see it as risk mitigation. The data actually shows that companies investing in this area see 3x ROI within 12 months. Here's how we'd mitigate that risk — we'll phase the investment over 2 quarters, so we can adjust based on early results. Are we aligned on starting with a small pilot?"

Requirements:

  • Use "I appreciate you raising that concern..."
  • Use "What if we approached it this way..."
  • Use "Here's how we'd mitigate that risk..."
  • Provide evidence when appropriate
