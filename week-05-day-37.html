Building a Persuasive Proposal

Week 6 không dạy persuasion. Week 6 dạy negotiation thinking. Hôm nay bạn sẽ học cách structure proposal sao cho người khác có thể evaluate và support. Strong proposals don't focus on what you want. They help the other person understand why it makes sense.

Today's Philosophy

From "Asking" to "Proposing"

Strong proposals don't focus on what you want. They help the other person understand why it makes sense.

4 Principles of Week 6: Understand before persuading • Interests before positions • Value before compromise • Commitment before agreement.

Today is about Move 1: Showing value. Help people see why it makes sense.

Why most proposals fail
  • Chỉ tập trung vào "what I want" thay vì "why it makes sense"
  • Không mention alternatives đã consider
  • Vague about what happens if we don't act
  • Không address trade-offs upfront
  • No clear recommendation
What C1 communicators do differently
  • Show the value clearly (benefit + timeframe)
  • Highlight the risk of inaction (what we lose by waiting)
  • Compare the options (show alternatives considered)
  • Make the recommendation with reasoning
  • Explain the trade-off honestly

5 Communication Moves

5 moves to build persuasive proposals. Each has register progression and Weak/Strong comparison. Click 🔊 to listen.

1
Show the Value
Core idea: "It'll cost [X], but we'll gain [Y] within [timeframe]."
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High
Opening any proposal
Decision-makers need to see value, not just cost. Value framing shifts the conversation from "Can we afford this?" to "Can we afford NOT to do this?"

Register Progression 🔊

🏠 Casual
"It'll cost more now, but it'll save us later."
Personal decisions
☕ Neutral
"The investment is around X, with expected benefits of Y."
Team discussions
💼 Professional
"This would take about X hours, but save us Y hours per month."
Colleagues, Managers
📋 More Formal
"The upfront cost is X, with projected savings of Y over Z period."
Senior leaders
⚠️ Weak → Strong
❌ Weak: "We need this new software." (Just asking)
✅ Strong: "The new software takes 2 weeks to learn, but it'll save our team 10 hours per week on reporting." (Value + timeframe)
2
Highlight the Risk
Core idea: "If we don't do this, we'll end up with [specific consequence]."
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High
Creating urgency
Specific, concrete consequences create urgency. Vague warnings don't.

Register Progression 🔊

🏠 Casual
"If we don't fix this now, we'll end up with..."
Personal decisions
☕ Neutral
"The downside of waiting is..."
Team discussions
💼 Professional
"If we don't act now, we'll likely face [specific problem]."
Colleagues, Managers
📋 More Formal
"Failure to act would result in [quantified loss]."
Senior leaders
⚠️ Weak → Strong
❌ Weak: "We should do this soon or we might have problems." (Vague, weak urgency)
✅ Strong: "If we don't update our process now, we'll end up with a backlog of 500 unresolved tickets by Q4." (Specific, concrete consequence)
3
Compare the Options
Core idea: "Here are the [X] options I considered..."
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High
Showing due diligence
Showing options demonstrates you've done your homework. It builds credibility and helps decision-makers understand why your recommendation is the best choice.

Register Progression 🔊

🏠 Casual
"We looked at a few options..."
Personal decisions
☕ Neutral
"We considered X, Y, and Z..."
Team discussions
💼 Professional
"Here are the 3 options I looked at..."
Colleagues, Managers
📋 More Formal
"We evaluated 4 options with the following trade-offs..."
Senior leaders
⚠️ Weak → Strong
❌ Weak: "This is the best option." (No comparison)
✅ Strong: "Here are the 3 options I considered. Option B balances cost with time best for our situation." (Shows due diligence)
4
Make the Recommendation
Core idea: "I recommend [X] because [specific reason tied to value]."
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High
Clear proposal
Clear reasoning tied to value helps decision-makers understand why this is the best choice.

Register Progression 🔊

🏠 Casual
"I think we should go with X because..."
Personal decisions
☕ Neutral
"I'd recommend X because..."
Team discussions
💼 Professional
"I recommend X because [clear reason]."
Colleagues, Managers
📋 More Formal
"Based on the analysis, I recommend X for the following reasons..."
Senior leaders
⚠️ Weak → Strong
❌ Weak: "I think we should do this." (No reasoning)
✅ Strong: "I recommend the new scheduling tool because it reduces manual work by 40% and integrates with our current systems." (Clear reasoning tied to value)
5
Explain the Trade-off
Core idea: "The trade-off is [what we gain] vs [what we give up]."
⭐⭐⭐⭐ High
Honest evaluation
Trade-offs show you understand there's no perfect solution. It demonstrates strategic thinking and helps decision-makers make informed choices.

Register Progression 🔊

🏠 Casual
"We get X, but we give up Y."
Personal decisions
☕ Neutral
"The trade-off is X vs Y."
Team discussions
💼 Professional
"The trade-off is [specific benefit] vs [specific cost/risk]."
Colleagues, Managers
📋 More Formal
"This approach involves trading [X] for [Y], with implications for..."
Senior leaders
⚠️ Weak → Strong
❌ Weak: "There are some pros and cons." (Vague)
✅ Strong: "The trade-off is moving closer to work vs the kids changing schools. We'd save 1 hour daily but they'd lose their current friends." (Specific, honest)

Integrated Dialogues

See how C1 speakers build persuasive proposals in real conversations. No labels in real life — just natural flow.

🏠 Dialogue 1: Proposing Hybrid Work Schedule for Family (Life)

Context: You and your spouse are discussing changing work schedules to better manage housework and childcare.
You:
"I've been thinking about our schedule situation. Can I walk you through what I've found?"
Spouse:
"Sure, what are you thinking?"
You:
"I think we should try a hybrid schedule where I work from home 3 days a week. The trade-off is less office face time vs more flexibility for school pickups and house stuff." Trade-off
Spouse:
"Will your boss be okay with that?"
You:
"Good question. Here are the 3 options I considered: Option A is keeping the current schedule. Option B is hybrid 3 days WFH. Option C is going fully remote. I recommend Option B because it gives us flexibility without losing team connection." Options Recommendation
Spouse:
"What happens if we don't change anything?"
You:
"If we don't adjust our schedule, we'll end up with the same stress we've had for 6 months — rushing for pickups, paying for after-school care, and feeling burnt out." Risk
Spouse:
"That's true. What's the value though?"
You:
"It'll save us about $400/month on after-school care and give us 5 more hours per week together as a family. The trade-off is I might need to be more intentional about team meetings, but we'll gain real flexibility." Value
Spouse:
"How do we try it out?"
You:
"My recommendation is we try it for 4 weeks and check in. If it doesn't work, we can always go back to the current schedule. Are we aligned on starting next Monday?" Recommendation
Spouse:
"Yeah, let's try it. Sounds good."

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "The trade-off is..." → Explain the Trade-off (Move 5)
  • "Here are the 3 options I considered..." → Compare the Options (Move 3)
  • "I recommend... because..." → Make the Recommendation (Move 4)
  • "If we don't adjust... we'll end up with..." → Highlight the Risk (Move 2)
  • "It'll save us... and give us..." → Show the Value (Move 1)
  • Register: Casual throughout (spouses)
  • 12 turns natural flow, building persuasive proposal for family decision

💼 Dialogue 2: Proposing New Process for Team (Business)

Context: You're a team lead wanting to propose a new process for handling client requests. Currently the team uses manual spreadsheets and it takes too much time.
Manager:
"You mentioned wanting to discuss our client request process. What's on your mind?"
You:
"Thanks for making time. I'd like to propose switching from spreadsheets to a ticketing system. Can I walk you through why?"
Manager:
"Go ahead."
You:
"It'll take about 2 weeks to set up and 1 week for the team to learn, but it'll save us about 15 hours per week on manual tracking and status updates." Value
Manager:
"That sounds good, but what's the risk of switching?"
You:
"The trade-off is upfront learning time vs long-term efficiency. Here are the 3 options I considered: Option A is keeping spreadsheets. Option B is the ticketing system I'm proposing. Option C is hiring an admin to manage the spreadsheets." Trade-off Options
Manager:
"What about Option C?"
You:
"Option C costs $3K/month ongoing. Option B is a one-time $500 setup with no ongoing cost. I recommend Option B because it's sustainable and the team becomes self-sufficient." Recommendation
Manager:
"What happens if we don't change?"
You:
"If we don't switch systems, we'll end up with the same bottleneck we have now — requests falling through the cracks and the team spending 30% of their time on admin instead of client work." Risk
Manager:
"What's the timeline?"
You:
"My recommendation is we start setup next week, have the team trained by end of month, and fully transition by mid-next month. The trade-off is a busy month now vs 15 hours saved per week going forward." Recommendation
Manager:
"Okay, let's do it. Send me the plan."
You:
"Perfect. I'll send over the implementation timeline by EOD. Are we aligned on starting Monday?"

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "It'll take... but it'll save..." → Show the Value (Move 1)
  • "The trade-off is..." → Explain the Trade-off (Move 5)
  • "Here are the 3 options I considered..." → Compare the Options (Move 3)
  • "I recommend... because..." → Make the Recommendation (Move 4)
  • "If we don't switch... we'll end up with..." → Highlight the Risk (Move 2)
  • Register: Professional (manager-employee)
  • 14 turns, complete persuasive proposal presentation

🎵 Intonation Patterns — When & Why

"It'll save us..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when stating benefits. Falling tone = confident, factual. Avoid: Rising (sounds uncertain about your own numbers).
"If we don't do this..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when highlighting risk. Falling tone = serious, important. Avoid: Too soft (làm mất sense of urgency).
"I recommend..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when making clear proposal. Falling tone = decisive, confident. Avoid: Rising (sounds like you're asking permission instead of proposing).

Speaking: 4 Scenarios

Practice building persuasive proposals in different contexts. Remember: no register is "better" — it's about appropriateness!

🟢 Casual - 3-4 min

Scenario 1: Proposing a Schedule Change to Partner

You want to change family schedule (wake up earlier, change dinner time, rearrange chores). Build persuasive proposal.

💡 Model Opening: "I've been thinking about our schedule. Can I walk you through what I've found?" 📖 Key Moves: Use "It'll save us..." or "We'll gain...", "If we don't change... we'll end up with...", "Here are the options I considered..."

Requirements:

  • Use "It'll save us..." hoặc "We'll gain..."
  • Use "If we don't change... we'll end up with..."
  • Use "Here are the options I considered..."
  • Keep it collaborative, not pushy
🔵 Neutral - 3-4 min

Scenario 2: Proposing New Software to Team

You want team to adopt a new tool (project management, communication, documentation). Build persuasive proposal.

💡 Model Opening: "I'd like to propose switching to a new tool. Can I walk you through why?" 📖 Key Moves: Use "It'll take X time, but it'll save us Y time", "The trade-off is...", "I recommend... because..."

Requirements:

  • Use "It'll take X time, but it'll save us Y time"
  • Use "The trade-off is..."
  • Use "I recommend... because..."
  • Address concerns upfront
🟣 Professional - 4-5 min

Scenario 3: Proposing New Process to Manager

You want to propose an improved process (workflow, handoff, review process, training). Build persuasive proposal.

💡 Model Opening: "I'd like to propose a change to our current process. Can I walk you through the business case?" 📖 Key Moves: Use "Here are the 3 options I considered...", "If we don't change... we'll end up with...", "The trade-off is..."

Requirements:

  • Use "Here are the 3 options I considered..."
  • Use "If we don't change... we'll end up with..."
  • Use "The trade-off is..."
  • Quantify value clearly (time, quality, efficiency)
🟠 Challenge - 5 min

Scenario 4: The Double Proposal

Morning: Propose schedule change with family (Casual). Afternoon: Propose adopt new tool with team (Professional).

💡 Model Opening (Life): "I've been thinking about our daily routine. Can I share what I've found?" 💡 Model Opening (Business): "I'd like to propose we try a new approach to [X]. Can I walk you through why?"

Requirements:

  • Shift register flexibly
  • Use different moves for each context
  • Each proposal has: Value → Risk → Options → Recommendation → Trade-off
  • Quantify value appropriately for each context

Communication Outcomes

You lead with value, not just cost
You've considered and presented alternatives
You address risks and trade-offs honestly
Your recommendation has clear reasoning
Decision-makers can say yes or no with confidence

Pro Tips

Lead with value: "It'll save us 10 hours per week" before "It takes 2 weeks to learn"
Show alternatives: Demonstrates you've done your homework
Be specific: "500 unresolved tickets" not "a lot of problems"
Address risks upfront: Shows you're thinking strategically
Make clear recommendation: Don't make them guess what you want
