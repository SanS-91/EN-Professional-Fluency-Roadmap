Week 6 không dạy persuasion. Week 6 dạy negotiation thinking. Hôm nay bạn sẽ học cách biến "tôi vs bạn" thành "chúng ta vs vấn đề". B2 speakers compromise and both sides lose a little. C1 speakers collaborate and both sides get what actually matters.

Today's Philosophy

From Compromise to Collaboration

Compromise (thỏa hiệp) thường có nghĩa là cả hai bên đều phải từ bỏ một phần điều mình muốn. C1 communicators don't just compromise. They look beneath the surface positions to find where interests align, and they expand the options so both sides win.

Important: Finding a win-win doesn't mean giving the other person everything they ask for. It means structuring the solution so that both sides' core interests are met. Sometimes you only need 1 move to shift the dynamic from "me vs you" to "us vs the problem".

Common Mistakes

3 lỗi phổ biến khi trying to create win-win. Tránh những lỗi này để giữ collaboration authentic.

Mistake 1: Splitting the difference too early

❌ "You want $100, I want to pay $50. Let's do $75." (Neither side is happy).
✅ "Why do you need $100? Why do I want to pay $50?" (Find the overlap first).
Splitting the difference without understanding interests means both sides lose value.

Mistake 2: Assuming a fixed pie

❌ "If you get the budget, I lose it." (Zero-sum thinking).
✅ "How can we structure this so both our projects get funded?" (Expand the pie).
Assuming resources are fixed prevents creative solutions that benefit both sides.

Mistake 3: Giving in to keep the peace

❌ "Fine, we'll do it your way." (Resentment builds).
✅ "I want to support you, but I need X to make this work for me too." (Honest collaboration).
Giving in isn't win-win. It's lose-win. And the "loser" will resent it later.

Integrated Dialogues

See how C1 speakers align interests in real conversations. No labels in real life — just natural flow.

🏠 Dialogue 1: Siblings Planning Holiday Care for Parents (Life)

Context: Two siblings arguing about where aging parents should stay for the holidays. One has a small apartment, the other lives far away.
You:
"Mom and Dad really want to be with us for the holidays, but my apartment is too small for them to stay over."
Sibling:
"Well, I can't fly them out to my place. The flights are too expensive and Dad's health isn't great for traveling."
You:
"It seems like we both want them to be comfortable and safe, but also feel included in the holiday." Find Overlap
Sibling:
"Exactly. I just don't want them to be alone."
You:
"Me neither. What if we looked beyond them staying at my place or flying to yours?" Expand Options
Sibling:
"What do you mean?"
You:
"What if we rented a large, accessible Airbnb just a few miles from my apartment? Here's how this helps both of us — they get their own space and don't have to fly, and we can all gather there for the big meals." Highlight Shared Value
Sibling:
"That's actually a great idea. But who pays for it?"
You:
"If I can handle the booking and the cooking, would you be open to splitting the cost of the rental 50/50?" Offer Trade
Sibling:
"That feels fair. Let's do it."
You:
"Awesome. Does this feel like a fair balance for both of us?" Check Fairness
Sibling:
"Yeah, it really does. Thanks for figuring this out."

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "It seems like we both want..." → Find the Overlap (Move 1)
  • "What if we looked beyond..." → Expand the Options (Move 3)
  • "Here's how this helps both of us..." → Highlight Shared Value (Move 2)
  • "If I can handle... would you be open to..." → Offer a Trade (Move 4)
  • "Does this feel like a fair balance..." → Check for Fairness (Move 5)
  • Register: Casual throughout (siblings)
  • 12 turns natural flow, all 5 moves used naturally

💼 Dialogue 2: Marketing vs. Engineering over Shared Resource (Business)

Context: Marketing Lead and Engineering Lead arguing over the only Senior Data Analyst's time.
Marketing:
"I need David for 20 hours this week to pull the campaign reports. We launch Monday."
Engineering:
"I can't spare David. He's building the dashboard for the Q3 product launch. If he stops, we miss the deadline."
Marketing:
"If we don't have the reports, the campaign fails."
Engineering:
"It seems our shared goal is making sure both the campaign and the product launch successfully in Q3 without burning out David." Find Overlap
Marketing:
"Yes, but how? We both need his time."
Engineering:
"What if we explored a third option beyond David just doing manual reports for you?" Expand Options
Marketing:
"Like what?"
Engineering:
"Here's how this creates mutual value — David spends 4 hours tomorrow building an automated script. It gives you real-time campaign data instantly, and frees him up to finish my dashboard." Highlight Shared Value
Marketing:
"Can he really automate it that fast?"
Engineering:
"Yes. If we can get you the automated dashboard by Wednesday, would you be open to releasing David from your manual requests for the rest of the week?" Offer Trade
Marketing:
"That actually saves me time in the long run. Deal."
Engineering:
"Great. Does this feel like a balanced approach for both teams?" Check Fairness
Marketing:
"Absolutely. Let's sync with David."

Analysis — Move Usage:

  • "It seems our shared goal is..." → Find the Overlap (Move 1)
  • "What if we explored a third option..." → Expand the Options (Move 3)
  • "Here's how this creates mutual value..." → Highlight Shared Value (Move 2)
  • "If we can get you... would you be open to..." → Offer a Trade (Move 4)
  • "Does this feel like a balanced approach..." → Check for Fairness (Move 5)
  • Register: Professional (cross-functional leads)
  • 13 turns, complete win-win negotiation
  • All 5 moves used naturally - because complex resource conflicts need full range

🎵 Intonation Patterns — When & Why

"It seems like we both want..." ↘ Falling Context: Used when finding overlap. Falling tone = sincere, collaborative. Avoid: Rising (sounds like you're questioning their wants).
"What if we looked beyond..." ↗ Rising Context: Used when expanding options. Rising tone = inviting, curious. Avoid: Falling (sounds like you're telling them what to do).
"Does this feel like a fair balance..." ↗↘ Rise-fall Context: Used when checking fairness. Rise-fall = genuine question, seeking confirmation. Avoid: Flat (sounds robotic or insincere).
